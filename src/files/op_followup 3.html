One way to engage students in deeper thinking about fractions as operators is to use manipulatives or drawings to have them visually demonstrate what happens during the multiplication process.  Cramer and Bezuk (1991) and Bezuk & Armstrong (1992) published a variety of visualization-based activities for students to engage in in order to both demonstrate and to build upon their understanding of fraction multiplication using counters and area models.  In these activities, students are provided with a set of counters or a blank rectangular area and are then asked to select or to shade in a number of counters or a space in the rectangle that represents a given fraction of the whole.  Next, they are asked to select a given fraction of their first selection.  For example, if a student is given eight counters and is asked to identify  of the set, they should select six counters.  If they are then asked to then find  of the  selection, they should pick three of the six counters that they chose.  They may verify that , and they did indeed end up with three of the original eight counters.  In this activity, teacher will want to look for the ways in which the students interact with the pieces at each stage.  If they still include the pieces that have been removed in the first round, then they are likely having trouble understanding the ways in which multiplying fractions stacks in that each round cuts out more and more of the original.

            Another option for formatively assessing students’ understanding of fractions as operators is to have them engage in problem posing activities in which they generate their own mathematical questions based on a prompt (Silver, 1994).  In this case, a teacher might provide a student with a scenario and ask them to write a question that someone would use fraction multiplication to solve.  As an example, let us say that one lap around a track is  of a mile.  If a student writes a question such as, “If Mary runs  laps around the track, how many miles did she run?”, then it is more likely that they understand the operator function of fractions than if they write a question such as, “How many laps would Alan need to run if he wanted to run 2 miles?”, which would be solved using fraction division.  Alternatively, students who are new to these ideas may be presented with both of these questions and asked which one represents a multiplicative relationship and to give a reason for their answer.  These types of problem-posing activities can help students focus in on the structural aspects of these operations (English, 1997) and also allows them to practice constructing arguments to defend their choice of problem (Common Core State Standards Initiative, 2010).
